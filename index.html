The Harmful Legacy of Redlining

Redlining is a social justice issue rooted in discrimination. The term redlining refers to the practice of denying services, such as loans or insurance to people and communities based on their race. Redlining has led to the exclusion of certain communities from equal access to housing and financial opportunities. This matters because it promotes systemic inequality which makes it harder for those redlined communities to build wealth and improve their lives. A way to bring light to this issue is through education. Through learning about redlining we can understand the basis of inequality better and be a step closer towards dismantling discriminatory systems. Another way to address redlining is by championing for policy changes that provide equal access to housing and financial services. It can include policies that encourage investment in underserved communities and regulations that make lending fair. Furthermore, community-based initiatives can also help address the effects of redlining. Among the things we can do are support organizations that give financial education and services to underserved communities and promote community led development projects that prioritize marginalized peoples needs. Ultimately, redlining requires a multifaceted approach that includes education, policy change, and community involvement.

Education

Public schools in the U.S. continue to use the mapping system based on students address to determine where they go to school which leads to an increase in school quality based on the zip code's income. In the article it states that “predominantly nonwhite school districts receive $23 billion less funding than majority white school districts, despite serving the same number of students.”. This leads to a decrease in quality of education because teachers are not provided with adequate funding for classroom supplies as well as updated resources such as technology and resources. The article goes on to give statistics about two different school districts in New York City. “In zip code 10021, home to Manhattan’s Upper East Side, the average household income is more than $115,000 and the population is 80% white. Public schools have an average math proficiency score of 84% (versus the New York public school average of 54%) and a reading proficiency score of 84% (versus the 54% statewide average).” This just further proves the inequalities in education due to redlining maps which were originally used for outlining areas with sizable Black populations in red ink on maps as a warning to mortgage lenders as a way to evaluate the riskiness of mortgages in cities. .

There were several governmental agencies and institutions that played a role in the creation and consistency of readlining. History and Present Day Impacts

This article examines the impact of the Home Owners' Loan Corporation maps that were created in response to the Great Depression, on the evolution of urban housing markets from 1940 to 2010. By using a variety of empirical approaches, including the use of counterfactual boundaries that experienced the same pre-existing trends . By defining these boundaries as A-D, D being the lowest-graded neighborhoods and A being the highest-graded neighborhoods. The patterns appear to continue throughout many years especially with major cities like San Francisco, Chicago, and New York. The study also shows that racial segregation along HOLC borders remains prevalent even today, nearly 75 years later. Leading to reduced credit access, higher borrowing costs, and disinvestment in poor urban neighborhoods. Economic and Financial Impact

Redlining has had continuous financial effects since its inception. Households in lower graded zones are usually predicted to earn less than households in higher graded zones. Also, to quote Savi, 'Being born into a poorly graded neighborhood decreases a child’s annual earnings by $15,000 near age 30, even when their parents’ income remains constant'. So, not only is there disparity between each zone's average household incomes, but the poor sometimes become poorer. Redlining can be described as economic segregation, a system that traps people in the financial status they were born into.
